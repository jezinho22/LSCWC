About in a flash

How to use in a flash

As long ago as the 1880s, Hermann Ebbinghaus was showing how sequences of information can be learned by being exposed to them and then recalling them, and this is what our flash cards enable. Ebbinghaus concluded that the most effective way to learn facts was to revisit them at intervals, which increase after each learning. So for best results, use our flashcards again after half a day, a full day, three days, 6 days, 12 days and 24 days. Or more often, if you like.

“Success is achieved by developing our strengths, not by eliminating our weaknesses” Marilyn vos Savant

Students, with supportive instruction, are capable of learning to develop and use a combination of phonological, orthographic and morphological strategies from the earliest years of school (Critten et al 2013, Daffern 2018, Treiman & Kessler 2014).

But not every word can be spelled with this knowledge, and many of us struggle with words with multiple “tricky bits”.

“I cannot write in English, because of the treacherous spelling. When I am reading, I only hear it and am unable to remember what the write word looks like” Albert Einstein

Research tells us that practising and learning words is likely to improve spelling competence, and if this is combined with the strategies above, the likely improvement is significant (Fisher et al, 2007). Therefore our flashcards provide cues about tricky bits and where morphology can also help.

“Ladies, if you want to know the way to my heart... good spelling and good grammar, good punctuation, capitalize only where you are supposed to capitalize, it's done” John Mayer

References:

Critten, Sarah & Pine, Karen & Messer, David (2013) Revealing children's implicit spelling representations, The British journal of developmental psychology. 31. pp.198-211

Daffern, Tessa (2018) Editorial skills: Spelling in N.M Mackenzie & J. Scull (Eds), Understanding and supporting young writers from birth to 8 (pp. 116-136). Oxfordshire: Routledge

Treiman, R., & Kessler, B. (2014) How children learn to write words. Oxford University Press.

How to use in a flash

About in a flash

  1. Go to the Practise page
  2. Insert your name and choose your list from the dropdown menu
  3. You've started!
  4. Read the word on the card aloud
  5. Read the broken down word, in its parts, aloud
  6. Read as many times as you like, actually
  7. Click the card to hide the word
  8. Try to write the word in the box
  9. Cards you get right are discarded from the pack. Cards with mistakes are moved to the bottom of the pack, and they'll come up again until you get them right: so the ones you need to practise harder and remember are the ones you see more
  10. You can check which words gave you trouble by looking at the Results page once all the cards have been discarded
